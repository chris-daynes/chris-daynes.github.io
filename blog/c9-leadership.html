How does the SCARF model apply to your Phase 0 experience so far? How will it apply to bootcamp?

The SCARF model incompasses Status, Certainty, Autonomy, Relatedness, and Fairness. As I was reading this chapter, the last thing I was thinking about was how this related to my experience in phase 0. I was thinking about individuls within society as a whole. You know like why someone would buy a fancy brand new car and lose 10's of thousands as soon as you drive it off the car yard. Well, that's status for you! Bit irrational.

The chapter in SIY turns status around to looking inward. I'm not sure that's what the SCARF model was/is all about. I thought that status was a percieved social status. Make someone happy by giving them a fancy title but no pay. Universities do this all the time with 'honerary lecturer' status. Then they ask you to work for no monetary reward hoping that the status would be enough!

So with regard to phase 0. I think that my status has gone down. My work collegues look at me like I am crazy doing this course. It's certainly not about the money! In saying this though I like to think that I am old enough to not give a damn about status externally. My car is an old Honda that I bought off one of my junior collegues! Status interanally looms always large though. I am my biggest critic. Probaly too much so.

Regards certainty, I am certain that I will learn a bucket load of great stuff. Am I certain that I ultimately with know how to apply it well? Well no. There is a certain amount of autonomy within phase 0 but this must be seen within the confines of the ciriculum. There is no perceived loss of autonomy which is the important factor at play. Relatedness has been interesting. We are a diverse group and my interactions so far have been via that slackboard. They have been positive but not personal. I look forward to meeting everybody in the flesh so to speak. Regards Fairness or disruption of, this has not entered my thoughts at any time during phase 0.

Have a difficult conversation. How did it go? (It's a valuable skill, but can take practice to develop!)

I've had many difficult conversations and always think hard about them before hand and reflect on them heavily afterwards. They are difficult and I believe like negotiation is a skill gained over a lifetime and many reflections. Interestingly, hard converstaions are often their own reward. Frequently in hindsight, you are glad to have had them. When I was younger I had a number that I was coerced into and regret them to this day. Again, life-long learning. Oh yes and getting comfortable engaging in and having difficult converstions is a must in marriage!
